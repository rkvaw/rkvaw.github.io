Thoughts

  • Post 1 — Debugging the UART

    A journey through troubleshooting UART communication issues and how I finally got it working.

    When my UART link kept dropping bytes, I started by checking the basics: baud rate mismatches, wiring, and ground reference. It turned out that a subtle grounding issue introduced noise on the line under certain loads.

    I implemented hardware debouncing and added small capacitors to smooth spikes. After isolating sections of the circuit, I could reliably replicate the error and observe the faulty behaviour on a logic analyzer.

    On the firmware side, retry logic and checksum verification were introduced so partial frames could be recovered without corrupting downstream state. That made the system much more robust to transient faults.

    Documentation and automated tests were the final step: a small fuzz tester injected timing jitter and byte drops which helped validate the fixes under stress.

  • Post 2 — Messing with UART

    Experiments, mistakes, and discoveries while learning the ins and outs of UART protocols.

    Experimenting is the fastest way to learn a protocol. I tried different parity and stop-bit configurations to see how legacy devices responded, and kept a log of compatibility quirks.

    I built a small bridge that converted UART to WebSocket so I could monitor frames in the browser. That tooling paid off — seeing timing visually helped locate framing errors.

    Some mistakes were educational: accidentally swapping TX/RX pins produced strange behaviour that looked like data corruption but was simply misrouting.

    In the end, a combination of better tooling, automated checks, and modular firmware made future experiments straightforward and safer.

    Sharing snippets and a reproducible test bench helped teammates quickly reproduce scenarios and contribute fixes.

  • Post 3 — Done with the UART

    Wrapping up my UART adventure and sharing key takeaways for future projects.

    After weeks of iterating, I consolidated the learnings into a checklist: verify grounds, confirm baud/format, use a logic analyzer, and add retry/CRC where appropriate.

    Refactoring the protocol layer to be testable allowed unit tests to validate framing and error handling without hardware attached, streamlining development.

    We also added a lightweight monitoring mode that emits diagnostic frames to help field debugging without halting production behaviour.

    These practices reduced field incidents significantly and made onboarding easier for new engineers touching the communication stack.
